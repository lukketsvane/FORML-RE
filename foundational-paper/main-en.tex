\noindent\textbf{Abstract}\quad
Why does a design object take one form rather than another? Function cannot be the answer; all chairs share the affordance of sitting, yet form varies enormously. We propose \formlaere{}, a formal design theory that unifies Stiny's shape algebra, Hatchuel and Weil's C-K theory, and Levin's multi-scale competence framework into a single generative equation: $\mathrm{Form} = SG(\nabla_{C(A)}\, L(c,t))$. The theory is structured as seven propositions and introduces \textit{care}; the dimensionality of the agent's cognitive cone; as the measure of design intelligence. We test the theory against 2,066 European chairs (1280--2024) from two museum collections with 2,204 mesh models. Twenty statistical analyses across 14 cross-validation subsets yield results consistent with the core predictions; no postulate is falsified. Three worked examples; the cantilever chair race (1925--28), Neri Oxman's Silk Pavilion, and the Great Green Wall; demonstrate the theory's explanatory reach from furniture history to continental-scale ecology. The theory yields seven falsifiable research programs.
